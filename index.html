The Range · the one habit that changes everything

THE PRE-SHOT ROUTINE

Every good golfer runs the same routine before every swing — read the lie, pick the club, see the shot. Great prompting is the same discipline. Three moves, every time, and vague requests become expert-level outputs. Set up memory once — your caddie, back in The Bag — and Move 1 gets shorter every swing.

Move 1

Read the lie

The situation today.

Give Claude what's specific to this shot — the product, the market, the deadline. If your caddie (memory) already knows who you are, skip the intro and go straight to today's context.

Move 2

Pick the club

Define the task.

Say exactly what you want it to do: "Draft the campaign brief," "Write 5 captions," "Tear down this competitor." One clear shot, not "help me with marketing."

Move 3

See the shot

Specify the rules.

Picture the landing: format, length, tone, audience. "One-page exec brief, premium voice, ready for HQ." Now it lands where you aimed.

All three moves, in one breathYou are a senior brand marketer for TaylorMade Golf launching the P-Series irons in Korea this season — precision-focused golfers, heavy KakaoTalk usage, Callaway pushing fitting bundles. Draft three campaign messages and a one-page launch brief. Premium, confident voice, ready for HQ review.
And the 4th move — commit & adjust

First answer 80% there? That's normal — it's a conversation, not a vending machine. Take a mulligan: "tighten to the top 3, add a Japan angle, make it punchier." You refine, you don't restart. (This is the master doc's RACI-P framework, re-cut as a routine you'll actually remember on the tee.)

Going Pro · 3 · trust, but verify

SIGN YOUR OWN SCORECARD

In golf you sign your own card — get it wrong and it's on you. Same here: Claude gets you 80% of the way fast, but you're the player of record. Pros build the check into the routine.

make it show its work

Ask for sources

"Where did each number come from?" "Cite the doc and the line." If it can't point to a source, treat it as a draft, not a fact.

pressure-test it

Have it check itself

"Argue the other side." "What would a skeptic challenge?" "What did you assume?" A second pass catches what a first draft misses.

you sign it

Own the last 20%

Numbers, names, claims, brand voice — your judgment is the value. Never forward what you haven't read.

⛳ The bar

Before it leaves your hands: would this run in front of leadership, unedited? If not, take one more swing. Accuracy is the whole game.
